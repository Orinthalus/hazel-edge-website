Nature
The land & wildlife

A home on the edge of things

Cumbria, border country, open skies and weather — and a landscape that shifts with every hour of light. The land around Hazel Edge is part of what you are coming for.

The setting

Border country, open skies

Hazel Edge sits on the northern edge of Cumbria, close to the Scottish border. The landscape here is open and uncluttered — wide fields, distant hills, and the kind of sky that makes you look up.

This is not a manicured countryside. It is real rural Cumbria: the weather comes in, the light changes constantly and the land has a character of its own. For guests who love the outdoors, it is the kind of place that is hard to leave.

The land immediately around Hazel Edge is being actively managed and restored — native trees planted, meadow areas returning, more habitat created. This is slow, patient work, but the results are already visible in the variety of birds, insects and plants that have found their way here.

"Wide views across the border country — the kind of landscape that makes you stop and look."

The view

A wide open valley visible from the cottage and garden — border hills in the distance, the sky taking up more space than the land. The kind of view that changes your mood.

The sky

The seasons

Each season brings something different — summer evenings that linger, autumn mist in the valley, winter skies that go on forever, spring arriving slowly and with conviction.

What you might encounter

Wildlife at Hazel Edge

We only mention what is genuinely likely. The list below reflects what we regularly see on and around the smallholding.

🦅

Birds of prey

Buzzards, kestrels and occasionally red kites circle the valley. Watch the thermals above the ridge on still days.

🦌

Deer & hares

Roe deer are regular visitors at dawn and dusk. Brown hares can be spotted in the open fields in quieter months.

🦇

Bats

Summer evenings bring pipistrelles and brown long-eared bats — best seen at dusk from the garden.

🐦

Garden birds

Goldfinches, chaffinches, long-tailed tits and treecreepers are regular visitors to the smallholding.

🦋

Butterflies

Meadow browns, gatekeepers and orange tips in summer. The restoring meadow areas have increased numbers noticeably.

🌸

Wildflowers

Ox-eye daisies, knapweed, ragged robin and bird's-foot trefoil in the meadow restoration areas in summer.

🦔

Small mammals

Hedgehogs, field voles and wood mice are around — Hazel the dog shows a keen interest in locating them.

🌿

Insects

Bumblebees, hoverflies, beetles and dragonflies near the wetter areas. The coppice planting has brought more variety.

Restoration in progress

Woodland & meadow

Since arriving at Liddel Lodge, we have been planting and restoring. Native woodland species — hazel, birch, rowan, alder and field maple — have gone in across several areas of the holding. It is slow work, but the trees are establishing well.

Meadow restoration takes even longer, but sections of rough grass are being managed to encourage wildflowers back. Less cutting, no fertiliser, more patience.

We have also planted willow coppice on wetter ground — a traditional, renewable way of producing firewood. The aim is to become self-sufficient in logs for both Hazel Edge and our own home over the long term. You can read more about this on our sustainability page.

For guests, this means staying somewhere that is genuinely connected to the land — not just surrounded by countryside, but part of a living, changing landscape.

Our sustainability story
Hazel's corner

The dog behind the name

Hazel — our border collie — came into our lives a few years after we arrived at Liddel Lodge. She has since become thoroughly embedded in the project, the YouTube channel and, inevitably, in the name of the cottage.

She takes her nature-watching seriously (particularly anything small and rustling), and has strong opinions about walks, mealtimes and who is sitting in her spot on the sofa.

She lives with us at Liddel Lodge, so guests at Hazel Edge will not encounter her in person — but you are welcome to follow her exploits on The View From the Clouds.

✦ ✦ ✦

Dark skies above Hazel Edge

Away from street lights and town glow, the night sky here is something genuinely worth staying up for. On clear nights you can see the Milky Way from the garden.

The best nights are still, cold and dry — check the forecast before bed. A blanket and a hot drink help.
